Games, AI, and Information Integrity

Designing Playable Solutions
Instructor: Yeonhee Cho (Johnny) Institution: USC School of Information Science Platforms: Miro, Figma, InVision

Course Overview

This is not simply a game design class. It is an applied information science course focused on integrity, AI, and social impact[cite: 102]. Students leave as intervention-minded information professionals capable of designing evidence-based solutions to AI-driven information threats.

Weekly Schedule & Modules

Click a week to unfold the Learning Objectives, Required Readings, and Assignments.

WEEK 1: What Is Information Integrity in the AI Era? +
Learning Objectives:
  • Define integrity (accuracy, completeness, consistency)[cite: 119].
  • Distinguish misinformation, disinformation, and malinformation[cite: 119].
Required Readings:

Wardle & Derakhshan (2017); Floridi et al. (2018); Wineburg & McGrew (2019)[cite: 119].

Assignment: Concept Map (Information Integrity, Misinformation, AI, and real-world case)[cite: 119].
Instructor Note: Spend lecture time stress-testing taxonomy with student examples[cite: 119].
WEEK 2: Why Does Misinformation Matter? Real-World Consequences +
Learning Objectives:
  • Analyze documented harms in health, politics, or finance[cite: 122].
  • Evaluate population vulnerability and systemic issues of equity[cite: 125].
Required Readings:

Roozenbeek & van der Linden (2019); Chesney & Citron (2019); Costanza-Chock (2020)[cite: 125].

Assignment: Real-World Impact Snapshot (300-word analysis of a documented incident)[cite: 125].
WEEK 3: AI & The Transformation of Information Risk +
Learning Objectives:
  • Explain how Gen-AI lowers production costs and increases scale[cite: 127].
  • Identify threat vectors: synthetic text, deepfakes, and algorithmic amplification[cite: 127].
Required Readings:

Graphika & Stanford Internet Observatory (2022); Re-read Wardle & Derakhshan[cite: 127].

Assignment: AI Risk Amplification Analysis (400 words on technical risk vs mitigation)[cite: 130].
WEEK 4: Reflection & Professional Responsibility +
Learning Objectives:
  • Synthesize personal frameworks for understanding information integrity[cite: 132].
  • Articulate professional responsibilities in AI-saturated environments[cite: 132].
MAJOR ASSESSMENT: AI Integrity Briefing (15%)
Record a 5-min briefing for a hypothetical employer explaining AI-era integrity[cite: 132].
WEEK 5: Why Games? Inoculation & Procedural Rhetoric +
Learning Objectives:
  • Explain theoretical basis using inoculation theory and procedural rhetoric[cite: 141, 142].
Readings: Bogost (2007); Salen & Zimmerman (2004); Cho et al. (2023) [cite: 151, 152, 153].
Assignment: Research-to-Mechanics Analysis[cite: 157].
WEEK 6: Play & Evaluate Workshop +
Activity: Play Bad News, Harmony Square, Go Viral, Cranky Uncle, or Troll Factory[cite: 167].
Assignment: Structured Evaluation Worksheet[cite: 167].
WEEK 7: Identifying AI-Era Gaps +
Learning Objectives: Identify threats NOT addressed by current games[cite: 174, 175].
MAJOR ASSESSMENT: Game Evaluation Report (15%)
Team-based 1,500-word comparative analysis[cite: 184, 185].
WEEK 8-10: Research & Low-Fi Prototyping +
Module: Researching AI problems, narrowing focus, and translating into a paper/card prototype[cite: 193, 195, 201].
Assignments: AI Risk Brief (W8); Design Rationale (W9); Low-Fi Submission (W10)[cite: 194, 199, 201].
WEEK 11-12: Iteration & Hi-Fi Development +
Module: Revising based on instructor feedback and preparing the polished digital prototype[cite: 207, 209].
MAJOR ASSESSMENT: Hi-Fi Prototype (20%)
Playable components + design narrative tracing mechanics to theory[cite: 212].
WEEK 13-14: User Testing & Integrity Reflection +
Module: External testing (think-aloud technique) and ethical impact analysis[cite: 215, 221].
Assignments: Data Log (W13); Integrity Impact Reflection (W14)[cite: 218, 221].
WEEK 15: Public Showcase +
Learning Objectives: Communicate the full design arc to a public audience[cite: 224].
MAJOR ASSESSMENT: Final Presentation (15%)
15-min presentation (including demo and Q&A)[cite: 224].

Academic Policies

Late Work: Weekly assignments accepted up to 48 hours late (50% credit). Major assessments require prior written approval[cite: 229, 230].

Generative AI: Tools may be used for research/brainstorming, but all final writing must be student-authored[cite: 232]. Disclosure is mandatory[cite: 233].

Accessibility: Materials available in accessible formats upon request via OSAS[cite: 235].

Alternatives: Students facing barriers to game production may substitute alternative interactive media (e.g., interactive infographics) with approval[cite: 237].
