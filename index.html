Games, AI, and Information Integrity:

Designing Playable Solutions

Instructor: Yeonhee Cho (Johnny)

PhD Candidate, Information Science

Format: 15-Week Semester

Platforms: Miro, Figma, InVision

Course Description

This course explores how games can be designed to address information integrity challenges, including misinformation and AI-related risks[cite: 100]. Students evaluate existing games and develop original prototypes to promote integrity in the AI era[cite: 101, 103].

Detailed Weekly Schedule
PHASE 1: Foundations (Weeks 1–4)
Week 1: What Is Information Integrity?
Objectives: Define integrity dimensions (accuracy, completeness, consistency) and distinguish between mis-, dis-, and malinformation[cite: 119].
Readings: Wardle & Derakhshan (2017); Floridi et al. (2018); Wineburg & McGrew (2019)[cite: 119].
Assignment: Concept Map - Connecting integrity, AI, and real-world cases[cite: 119].
Week 2: Why Does Misinformation Matter?
Objectives: Analyze documented harms in health, politics, or finance and connect them to systemic issues of equity[cite: 121, 125].
Readings: Roozenbeek & van der Linden (2019); Costanza-Chock (2020)[cite: 125].
Assignment: Real-World Impact Snapshot - 300-word structured analysis[cite: 125].
Week 3: AI & Information Risk Transformation
Objectives: Explain how Gen-AI lowers the cost of misinformation and identify AI-amplified threat vectors[cite: 127].
Readings: Graphika & Stanford Internet Observatory (2022); Floridi et al. (2018) [cite: 127, 130].
Assignment: AI Risk Amplification Analysis - 400-word technical analysis[cite: 130].
Week 4: Professional Responsibility
Objectives: Synthesize frameworks for understanding integrity and articulate the responsibilities of information scientists[cite: 132].
MAJOR ASSESSMENT: AI Integrity Briefing (15%) - 5-min recorded briefing for a non-specialist audience[cite: 132].
PHASE 2: Play & Critique (Weeks 5–7)
Week 5: Why Games? Inoculation Theory
Objectives: Explain the theoretical basis for games using inoculation theory and procedural rhetoric[cite: 141, 142].
Readings: Roozenbeek & van der Linden (2019); Bogost (2007); Cho et al. (2023) [cite: 148, 151, 154].
Assignment: Research-to-Mechanics Analysis - Mapping theory to game mechanics[cite: 157, 158].
Week 6: Misinformation Games Workshop
Objectives: Play and evaluate games (e.g., Bad News, Harmony Square) using a structured evaluation protocol[cite: 164].
Assignment: Structured Evaluation Worksheet for three selected games[cite: 167].
Week 7: AI-Era Gap Identification
Objectives: Identify threats not addressed by existing games and formulate a design gap statement[cite: 172, 175, 177].
MAJOR ASSESSMENT: Game Evaluation Report (15%) - 1,500-word team comparative analysis[cite: 184, 185].
PHASE 3: Research & Prototyping (Weeks 8–12)
Week 8: Researching the Problem Ecosystem
Objectives: Map actors, mechanisms, and victims involved in the team's chosen AI misinformation challenge[cite: 194].
Assignment: AI Risk Brief (Team) - 600-word structured problem brief[cite: 194].
Week 9: Narrowing the Focus
Objectives: Define target audience, learning outcomes, and ethical design constraints[cite: 196, 199].
Assignment: Design Rationale Document - Connecting evidence to mechanics[cite: 199].
Week 10: Low-Fidelity Prototyping
Objectives: Translate rationale into a playable low-fi prototype and conduct internal team playtests[cite: 201].
Assignment: Low-Fidelity Prototype Submission (Photos/Specs)[cite: 201, 205].
Week 11: Iteration & Feedback
Objectives: Act on instructor feedback to revise design problems and document iteration using reflection-in-action[cite: 207].
Assignment: Iteration Memo - Evidence-based design changes[cite: 207].
Week 12: High-Fidelity Development
Objectives: Produce a complete, playable hi-fi prototype and write a structured testing protocol[cite: 209].
MAJOR ASSESSMENT: Hi-Fi Game Prototype (20%) - Prototype + Design Narrative[cite: 212].
PHASE 4: Test & Reflect (Weeks 13–15)
Week 13: User Testing & Data Collection
Objectives: Conduct external user testing sessions and document usability issues and learning outcomes[cite: 215].
Assignment: User Testing Plan & Data Log from at least two sessions[cite: 218].
Week 14: Integrity Impact Analysis
Objectives: Evaluate how the prototype advances integrity goals and honestly assess ethical risks[cite: 221].
Assignment: Integrity Impact Reflection - 700-word evidence-based synthesis[cite: 221].
Week 15: Public Showcase
Objectives: Communicate the full design arc and respond to peer/instructor questions with specificity[cite: 224].
MAJOR ASSESSMENT: Final Showcase Presentation (15%) - 15-min public presentation[cite: 224].
Grading & Policies

Process documentation and iteration account for 65% of the grade[cite: 110]. Late work for major assessments is not accepted without prior approval[cite: 230]. Students may use generative AI as a brainstorming aid, but all writing must be their own work[cite: 232].
