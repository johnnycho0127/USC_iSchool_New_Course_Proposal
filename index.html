Course Description

This course explores how games can be designed to address information integrity challenges, particularly misinformation and AI-generated content such as deepfakes and synthetic media. Students critically evaluate existing misinformation games and develop original, evidence-informed game prototypes that promote information integrity.

This is not a traditional game design course. It is an applied information science course focused on AI ethics, intervention design, and social impact. Students move from understanding AI-driven information risks → evaluating current interventions → designing and testing their own playable solutions.

Course Rationale

AI technologies have transformed the information ecosystem. Deepfakes, AI-generated text, synthetic audio, and automated content systems present new challenges to information integrity. Traditional media literacy emphasizes access, evaluation, and critical thinking — yet a gap persists between knowledge and behavioral resistance to misinformation. This course bridges that gap by:

  • Using games as experiential learning tools
  • Engaging students in intervention design
  • Integrating ethics, research, and systems thinking

Students become intervention-minded information professionals capable of designing tools that promote integrity in the AI era.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

  • 1. Analyze AI-driven information risks and misinformation systems.
  • 2. Critically evaluate misinformation games using theoretical frameworks.
  • 3. Identify gaps in current AI misinformation interventions.
  • 4. Design and prototype an evidence-informed playable intervention.
  • 5. Assess ethical implications and societal impact of their designs.
  • 6. Reflect on design decisions using structured documentation and research evidence.

Major Assignments
📌 Key principle: The game prototype is 20% of the grade. Process documentation and critical reflection carry 80%.
1. Weekly Assignments (Individual) — 30%

Short structured submissions (concept maps, risk analyses, evaluation worksheets, research briefs, reflection memos) submitted each week. Each assignment is worth 2% of the final grade.

2. AI Integrity Briefing (Individual) — 15%

Students create a recorded mini-presentation synthesizing key concepts from Weeks 1–4. Focuses on conceptual clarity, systems thinking, and AI risk analysis.

Rubric
Conceptual Understanding 30% Systems & AI Risk Analysis 30% Use of Course Material 20% Reflection & Clarity 20%
3. Misinformation Game Evaluation Report (Team) — 15%

Teams prepare a collaborative analytical report evaluating existing misinformation games using theoretical frameworks and empirical research covered in Weeks 5–7.

Rubric
Theoretical Integration 25% Analytical Depth 30% Evidence Use 20% AI-Era Gap Identification 15% Organization & Clarity 10%
4. AI-Misinformation Game Prototype (Team) — 20%

Teams design and develop a playable game-based intervention addressing a specific AI-driven misinformation challenge. Deliverables include a problem framing document, low-fidelity prototype, iteration memo, and high-fidelity prototype.

Rubric
Problem & Integrity Alignment 25% System Design & Mechanics 25% Evidence-Informed Iteration 20% Theoretical Integration 15% Ethical & Societal Awareness 15%
5. Public Presentation + Reflection (Individual) — 15%

Students present their team's intervention publicly and submit an individual analytical reflection evaluating effectiveness, limitations, and ethical implications of the game prototype.

Rubric
Evidence-Based Evaluation 30% Conceptual Integration 25% Ethical & Systems Awareness 25% Analytical Clarity 20%
6. Participation & Engagement (Individual) — 5%

Active engagement in discussions, peer feedback sessions, and in-class workshops throughout the semester.

Teaching Approach

This course is workshop-based and discussion-driven, structured around Kolb's experiential learning cycle: Abstract Conceptualization → Active Experimentation → Concrete Experience → Reflective Observation. A typical class session includes:

  • Short lecture (15–20 min) — concept grounding and framing
  • Gameplay or case exploration — hands-on experience with misinformation interventions
  • Small-group analysis — structured critique using theoretical frameworks
  • Design workshop — iterative prototyping and peer review
  • Structured reflection — individual or paired exit memos
Academic Integrity & Ethical Design

Because this course directly engages with misinformation and AI systems, ethical design is a course-wide commitment. Students must approach all design work ethically. Week 10 includes a mandatory ethical "red-team" exercise in which teams test whether their game could function as a manipulation tutorial. Designs must not facilitate harm. Proper citation of all sources is required.

Flexibility & Accessibility

If a team struggles to build a fully playable game, alternative interactive media formats may be approved (e.g., interactive comic, infographic-based system, narrative simulation, short video). The core objective is thoughtful, research-informed intervention design — not technical polish. Students are encouraged to continue developing prototypes beyond the course for civic, research, or portfolio purposes.

Career Relevance

Students leave with a portfolio-ready prototype, structured design documentation, and applied intervention experience. This course prepares students for roles in:

  • Civic technology and misinformation response
  • Journalism and media organizations
  • Library programming and information services
  • Public health communication
  • AI ethics consulting
  • Education technology
  • Information policy and government
